Latent Variables

Introduction

What is a latent variable?

  • A characteristic that cannot be measured directly
    • Political ideology
    • Support for the environment
    • Intelligence
  • Must be inferred from other observable variables
  • Central concept in social sciences

Example: Measuring Intelligence

  • Intelligence is a multidimensional concept
  • Each branch represents a different facet
  • Tests must capture this complexity

Measurement Challenge

Why is it complex?

  1. Abstraction: Concepts are often abstract
  2. Multidimensionality: Multiple aspects to consider
  3. Subjectivity: Varying interpretation depending on the context

A Suspicious Question

Does this question really measure history proficiency?

Or does it measure something else?

  • Pop culture?
  • Knowledge of cigars?

What’s Wrong

  • The best students mysteriously fail
  • Struggling students suddenly excel
  • The question does not measure history proficiency

Factor Analysis Compares All Questions

How does it work?

Factor analysis checks:

  1. The correlation between questions
  2. The pattern of answers
  3. The questions that “stand out”

Our results

  • Questions 1-4: ✅
    • Causes of the war
    • Operation Barbarossa
    • Economic impact
    • Yalta Agreement
  • Question 5: ❌
    • Churchill’s cigars
  • The first 4 questions measure history proficiency well
  • The cigar question measures something else (Knowledge of cigars? Pop culture?)
  • Factor analysis automatically detects these patterns

In Summary

A good history question should:

  • Test historical understanding
  • Show a “normal” distribution
  • Discriminate based on proficiency

A problematic question can:

  • Test irrelevant knowledge
  • Show a reversed distribution
  • Not reflect the targeted proficiency

Factor Analysis

Three Statistical Measures

  1. Cronbach’s Alpha
  2. First Eigenvalue
  3. Factor Loading

Cronbach’s Alpha

What is it?

  • A measure of the internal consistency of a set of questions
  • Indicates whether the questions measure the same thing
  • Varies between 0 and 1 (closer to 1 is better)

Simple Example

  • Imagine a job satisfaction survey with 4 questions:
    • “I like my job”
    • “I am happy at the office”
    • “My job satisfies me”
    • “I thrive in my work”
  • If answers are consistent with each other → High Alpha
  • If answers are contradictory → Low Alpha

In Practice

  • Alpha > 0.6: Acceptable
  • Alpha > 0.7: Good
  • Alpha > 0.8: Excellent

The First Eigenvalue

What is it?

  • Indicates the strength of the main concept measured by your questions
  • The higher it is, the more your questions measure a single, strong concept

Simple Analogy

5 questions to measure healthy lifestyle habits:

  • “I never eat refined sugar”
  • “I prioritize organic products”
  • “I go to the gym 4 times a week”
  • “I take protein supplements”
  • “I own a smartwatch to track my cardio”

In Practice

  • Value > 1: Acceptable
  • The higher the value, the more reliable it is
  • Indicates that there is a “dominant factor” in your questions

Factor Loading

What is it?

  • Measures the contribution of each question to the overall concept
  • Indicates whether each question is relevant

Simple Example

  • For a survey on “love of cats”:
    • “Cats are soft” → High loading (relevant)
    • “I eat pasta” → Low loading (irrelevant)

In Practice

  • Coefficient > 0.3: Acceptable
  • Allows identifying which questions to keep or remove
  • The higher the coefficient, the more relevant the question

The Three Measures Work Together

  • Cronbach’s Alpha: Overall consistency
  • First Eigenvalue: Strength of the main concept
  • Factor Loading: Relevance of each question

Practical Use

  1. Cronbach’s Alpha: > 0.6
  2. First Eigenvalue: > 1
  3. Factor Loading: > 0.3

  2. NO missing values
  3. The same measurement scale for all questions (0-1)

Validity?

How to measure it?

  1. Agreement with theory
  • Do the questions measure the concept well?
  • Are the results consistent with the literature?
  1. Agreement with other measures
  • Are the results similar to other scales?
  • Are the results stable over time?

The Grammar of Graphics

Graphical Representation of Data

General Principles of Visualization

  • Visualize data

  • Avoid unnecessary distractions

  • Avoid “spaghetti” charts

  • Avoid overly complex, overlapping, and intertwining lines

  • Start in black and white and use colors effectively

Basic Components

Visualization with ggplot2

Initialize a chart

ggplot(df, aes(x = Agriculture, y = Fertility, color = Education))
  • df is the dataframe
  • aes() is the function used to specify the variables to use
  • x and y are the variables to use for the x and y axes
  • color is the variable to use for the color

Visualization with ggplot2

Add a geom_()

ggplot(df, aes(x = Agriculture, y = Fertility, color = Education)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.8) # alpha is transparency
  • There are several geom_() functions for different types of charts
    • geom_point() for a scatter plot
    • geom_line() for a line chart
    • geom_bar() for a bar chart
    • geom_histogram() for a histogram

Visualization with ggplot2

Add a color scale

ggplot(df, aes(x = Agriculture, y = Fertility, color = Education)) +
  geom_point(alpha = 0.8) + # alpha is transparency
  scale_color_gradient(low = "blue", high = "red", name = "Education")
  • scale_color_gradient() allows specifying colors for the Education variable
  • low and high are the colors for the lowest and highest values
  • name is the name of the legend
  • You can use hex codes for colors

Visualization with ggplot2

Linear Regression

\[ \begin{aligned} y &= \beta_0 + \beta_1x + \epsilon \\ \end{aligned} \]

model <- lm(df$y ~ df$x, data = df) # A single line of code!

What is it?

  • A tool for understanding the relationship between two variables
  • Allows predicting one variable from another
  • Finds the “best line” representing this relationship

Visualizing the Regression

How does it work?

Regression finds the line that fits “best” through the points. The closer the points are to the line, the better the regression.

Why use regression?

  • Understand relationships between variables
  • Predict future values
  • Test scientific hypotheses
